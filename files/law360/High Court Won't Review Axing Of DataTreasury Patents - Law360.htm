High Court Won't Review Axing Of DataTreasury Patents

By Kevin Penton

Law360, New York (March 20, 2017, 6:09 PM EDT) -- The U.S. Supreme Court declined Monday to hear a bid by DataTreasury for it to review whether the Federal Circuit erred by invalidating two check-imaging patents as abstract under the high court’s landmark Alice decision.

The justices did not expand on their rationale for denying the petition for certiorari filed in January by DataTreasury Corp., which alleged that the Federal Circuit erred by invalidating the asserted claims as abstract and not inventive under the Supreme Court’s 2014 ruling in Alice Corp. v. CLS Bank International, in which the high court held that abstract ideas implemented through a computer are patent-ineligible under Section 101 of the Patent Act.

Counsel for the parties could not be reached for comment Monday.

Fidelity National Information Services Inc. challenged DataTreasury’s patents before the Patent Trial and Appeal Board under the America Invents Act business method review in November 2013, arguing that the asserted claims should be tossed because they recite abstract ideas while adding only routine, conventional features and using standard components to perform their functions.

Lawmakers had cited DataTreasury and its assertion of the patents against a slew of banks and other financial institutions over the years — during which it reportedly accrued more than $350 million in settlement payments — in the legislative leadup to the creation of the business method review under the 2011 law.

The patents cover the methods for acquiring images from financial documents, then transmitting and storing the images at a central facility, according to court documents.

Upon review, the PTAB said in April 2015 that each of the contested claims was invalid as an abstract idea. Additionally, it found certain claims were unpatentable for a lack of written description.

During oral arguments before the Federal Circuit in October, DataTreasury argued that the PTAB incorrectly concluded that the patents could be reviewed under the covered business method program. While patents subject to the review are defined as having claims used in the practice, administration or management of a financial product or service, DataTreasury’s cover “technological inventions,” the company argued.

“The reason that these patents do claim technological inventions is because they have a novel network architecture that uses an encryption-based process to solve a technical problem, mainly how computers efficiently process and manage large volumes of information,” Christian Hurt of Nix Patterson & Roach LLP told the appellate court panel.

On Oct. 13, the Federal Circuit upheld the PTAB’s decision in a one-sentence order.

The patents-in-suit are U.S. Patent Numbers 5,910,988 and 6,032,137.

DataTreasury is represented by Nelson J. Roach of Nix Patterson & Roach LLP.

Fidelity is represented by Erika H. Arner of Finnegan Henderson Farabow Garrett & Dunner LLP.

The case is DataTreasury Corp. v. Fidelity National Information Services Inc., case number 16-883, in the Supreme Court of the United States.
