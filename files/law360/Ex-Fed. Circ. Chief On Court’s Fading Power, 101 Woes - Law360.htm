Ex-Fed. Circ. Chief On Court’s Fading Power, 101 Woes

By Erin Coe

Law360 (March 30, 2018, 11:45 AM EDT) -- It’s been almost four years since Randall Rader gave up his leadership post at the Federal Circuit and promptly retired from the bench amid a flap over an email he sent flattering an appellate litigator’s work. The controversy changed the course of his career, and for that, Rader remains forever grateful, he recently told Law360 in an exclusive interview.

Rader, who served on the Federal Circuit for more than two decades and led the court as chief judge from 2010 to mid-2014, has been a vocal advocate for strong patents and was the first to refer to the validity proceedings before the Patent Trial and Appeal Board as “death squads” for patents.

Randall Rader speaks at a U.S. intellectual property litigation seminar for Chinese IP professionals in Shenzhen, China, in 2017.

As a judge, he tried to inject economic analysis into patent damages law. In Cornell University v. Hewlett-Packard Co. in 2009, for instance, Rader cut damages awarded to Cornell from $184 million to $71.2 million, finding that the royalty base used for the original award was overbroad because it didn’t show that the patented invention drove demand for the product.

While he was the leader of the Federal Circuit, the appellate court waded into several hotly debated areas, like inequitable conduct and patent eligibility, and reaching some kind of consensus proved elusive at times. He still considers the Federal Circuit’s 2013 fractured en banc decision in CLS Bank International v. Alice Corp. Pty. Ltd., which held that Alice’s computerized platform for reducing risk in financial trading was merely an abstract concept that couldn’t be patented, one of his greatest judicial failures. The U.S. Supreme Court affirmed the ruling in 2014.

That same year, Rader came under fire for sending a Weil Gotshal & Manges LLP attorney an email praising his performance at an oral arguments as "impressive in every way" and encouraging him to spread the message. After The Wall Street Journal reported in May 2014 that Rader had sent the email, he resigned as chief judge the next day and issued a public apology calling his behavior an “inexcusably careless” breach of ethics. The judge said he regretted sending the email, which he said could give the impression that the attorney was in a position to influence his thinking. He retired from the court entirely soon after.

His abrupt departure left some observers scratching their heads that the offense didn’t warrant such a drastic step. But when Rader reflects on that pivotal moment, he told Law360 recently that he’s glad it happened because it gave him a chance to fulfill a larger destiny.

“The important thing I take away is what a wonderful opportunity it has given me,” he said. “I look back on it with gratitude.”

If it hadn’t been for that email misstep, Rader acknowledged that he probably would still be at the Federal Circuit. But instead, Rader is now busy focusing on arbitration and mediation, litigation counseling and patent valuation at The Rader Group PLLC, while also getting ready to launch an arbitration center in Tokyo this summer.

“The two greatest professional events in my life were joining the Federal Circuit and leaving the Federal Circuit,” he said. “I’ve greatly profited from and enjoyed the work I’m doing. … It’s turned out greater than I had expected.”

While Rader may have left the bench, he still has plenty of strong opinions about how the Federal Circuit, the PTAB and the U.S. patent system are doing, and he didn’t hold back from sharing them with Law360.

Federal Circuit Losing Influence

The Federal Circuit used to be a leader that guided the world on intellectual property law, but it’s falling back into a more perfunctory role, in part because it is being inundated by appeals from the PTAB, according to Rader.

“The majority of the work is administrative validity work coming from the PTAB,” he said. “It’s kind of sad to see that the Federal Circuit is not as powerful of a tool in the shaping of the markets and market destiny as it once was.”

The Federal Circuit’s diminished position also likely has to do with the Supreme Court’s sweeping efforts to restructure patent law away from the statute toward its own concept of what the law should be as well as with competition coming from courts abroad, he said. While the world once turned to the Federal Circuit for answers to IP problems, it is now looking to other sources, like the U.K. trial and appellate courts, the Tokyo High Court and the emerging court system in China, he said.

“Leadership in IP law is shifting away from the U.S. to Asia, where they do more cases, and they do them with great precision and care,” he said. “Even U.S. companies are recognizing the value of litigating outside the U.S.”

The Patent Eligibility Dilemma

The case law for Section 101 of the Patent Act has narrowed what subject matter is eligible for patents, a move that has been disruptive and created uncertainty for patentees, particularly software and biotech companies that are the biggest sources of innovation, according to Rader.

“The law of 101 is not followed, and the statute is not obeyed,” he said. “It’s now a series of judge-made — I wouldn’t even call them rules — I’d just call them judge-made outcomes. It’s so embarrassing as I teach in Munich, Seoul or China. I have to say, ‘Don’t follow U.S. law. Your law is closer to the U.S. statute than the U.S. case law is to the statute.’”

U.S. universities tend to be hotbeds of invention, but as they continue to face difficulties patenting their innovations, they will be forced to shift research and development abroad where they can get better protection, he said.

While the U.S. is already starting to see this shift with a drop in patent litigation and patent law firms no longer needing as many big litigation teams, Rader says he also noticed it in the U.S. patent law class he taught last semester at The George Washington University Law School, where out of about 100 students, more than half of them were from foreign countries like China, India, Japan, South Korea, Europe and South America.

“The foreign students are hungry for IP education,” he said. “Meanwhile, there are fewer American students because there are fewer jobs as litigation goes down and because the general impression is IP is no longer the hot market it once was.”

A ‘Great Disappointment’

When Rader thinks back on the Federal Circuit’s en banc Alice decision in 2013, he wishes the appeals court had done a better job of taking a clear stand on what Section 101 says — that almost all subject matter is patent eligible. But instead the appeals court issued a one-paragraph per curiam decision affirming a trial court's decision that each of the four patents Alice asserted against CLS Bank were ineligible — followed by six strongly conflicting opinions on how much protection the patent system should afford software. The Federal Circuit and ultimately the Supreme Court adopted and followed judicial exceptions to the statutory standard, according to Rader.

“I felt great disappointment with the result, and that [ruling] continued to be a disruptive feature of the U.S. patent system,” he said.

Since that ruling, Rader said Federal Circuit judges really aren’t pointing out the problem that the Section 101 case law is veering from the statute, with the exception of a concurrence by Federal Circuit Judge Richard Linn in the decision in Ariosa Diagnostics Inc. et al. v. Sequenom Inc.

In that case, Judge Linn said he only agreed with his colleagues that the patent claims of the “breakthrough” prenatal DNA test should be invalidated because he was bound by the “sweeping language” in Mayo Collaborative Services v. Prometheus Laboratories Inc., where the Supreme Court created a two-part test for distinguishing patents that claim laws of nature and abstract ideas from those that claim patent-eligible applications of those concepts.

In the approximately 120 subject matter eligibility rulings since the Alice decision, Rader would have expected to see dozens of dissents by Federal Circuit judges saying that the case law doesn’t follow the statute.

“Instead there’s just a devastating silence,” he said. “The Supreme Court has emphasized the exceptions, and the lower courts act in an obedient way rather than perhaps staying dedicated to the statute.”

The Federal Circuit recently has issued decisions that may help patent owners keep their patents from being invalidated early on in a case, but Rader was not all that encouraged by the trend, saying the favorable decisions for patent owners serve as “little marginal corrections” that only add another layer of complexity to the whole system.

If the Supreme Court doesn’t address the problem, Congress should, though established businesses are expected to fight any reform attempts, he said.

“You have strong forces who would prefer to have fewer challenges to their dominance of the market from new patent owners,” Rader said.

PTAB Needs To Change

As for the PTAB, which has received thousands of patent validity challenges and been criticized for invalidating a wave of patents over the years, Rader hasn’t softened his view of the proceedings as “death squads.”

“That’s their job,” he said. “They review patent validity on narrow grounds, and they do their job well.”

Last year, Rader expressed interest in serving as director of the USPTO and vowed to “make patents great again.” If he had been chosen to lead the agency, Rader said his top priority would have been reforming the PTAB, and he would have started by making the procedures better emulate those in district court.

“Perhaps the biggest thing an issued patent deserves is a presumption of validity,” he said. “Having a clear and convincing error standard would be a step in the right direction.”

A New Dispute Resolver

In the meantime, Rader is busy providing arbitration, mediation and legal consulting services at The Rader Group, the Alexandria, Virginia-based firm he founded in 2014. He also teaches IP courses at The George Washington University Law School in Washington, D.C., Tsinghua University School of Law in Beijing and at other law schools around the globe.

One of the biggest projects he’s working on at the moment is creating an arbitration facility with an emphasis on IP disputes, the International Arbitration Center in Tokyo, after the Japanese government asked for his assistance. He will serve as the center’s head commissioner and has already recruited retired patent judges from China, South Korea, Europe, Japan and the U.S. to serve as arbitrators.

Rader has high hopes for the center, which aims to have a one-year default time limit on arbitration proceedings and an appeal procedure for “major” mistakes. The center will hold a mock arbitration demonstration in Tokyo on June 29 and is set to begin hearing proceedings in August.

“I’m creating what I hope will be the most efficient and proficient resolver of disputes in the world,” he said.

A Biking Life

When Rader is not working or trying to catch some shut-eye on a transatlantic plane trip, he might be found climbing up Mount Vernon in Fairfax County, Virginia, on his bike or making the 50-mile ride from Beijing to the Great Wall of China.

A few years ago, he biked about 1,000 miles on a yearlong trek from Washington, D.C., to South Carolina and back by himself. He’d bike all day, call up a nearby hotel to reserve a room, wash his clothes in the sink, sleep and then get up and do it all over again.

“I just had the clothes on my back and my phone,” he said.

Although Rader doesn’t get to bike as much as he’d like to these days, he still appreciates the benefits he gets when he does.

“It’s a time when I can think through everything,” he said. “It clears your head.”
