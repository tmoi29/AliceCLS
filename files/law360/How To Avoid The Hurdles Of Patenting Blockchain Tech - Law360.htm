How To Avoid The Hurdles Of Patenting Blockchain Tech

By Matthew Bultman

Law360 (March 9, 2018, 7:38 PM EST) -- There is a race to file patents for improvements to blockchain, the ledger technology underlying bitcoin, as fervor around the technology continues to build, but experts warn of potential stumbling blocks that could trip up the unwary inventor.

The number of patent filings related to blockchain and distributed ledger technology have grown at an exponential rate, as companies continue to pour resources into the development of the technology as it is adapted across a range of industries.

One recent analysis, conducted by attorneys at Rimon PC, showed there were over 740 blockchain patent publications worldwide in September, a 300 percent increase from January 2016.

“It’s a race to the patent office today,” Thomas Franklin of Kilpatrick Townsend & Stockton LLP said. “If you’re not figuring out how blockchain works for your business and writing those patents, someone else is.”

Here is a look at some potential obstacles to consider when seeking blockchain patents — and how to overcome them.

Keeping Alice in Mind

Blockchain is a shared ledger for recording the history of transactions that is maintained by a network of computers. The benefits of this technology — such as an immutable record and distributed control over the ledger — extend to applications well beyond cryptocurrencies.

One hurdle for inventors is drafting a blockchain application that will survive scrutiny under the U.S. Supreme Court’s decision in Alice Corp. v. CLS Bank. The 2014 case held that abstract ideas implemented on a computer are not patent-eligible under Section 101 of the Patent Act.

The Supreme Court has laid out a two-part test for determining patent eligibility: Are the claims at issue directed to an abstract idea? And, if so, do they include something "significantly more" than the abstract idea itself?

“One way to overcome challenges under [Section 101] would be to ensure that the claims are directed to an inventive concept beyond just the algorithm that underlies the blockchain, which could potentially be considered an abstract idea,” Andrew Riley of Goodwin Procter LLP said.

The Federal Circuit in Bascom v. AT&T ruled that a patent covering a system for filtering internet content was directed to an abstract idea. But it also held that the filtering schemes and ISP servers making up the system were combined in an inventive way that transformed that idea into something that was patent-eligible.

Thomas Isaacson of Polsinelli PC said that is a case those drafting a blockchain-based application can learn from.

“There is a distributed nature to the filtering invention in Bascom that can be analogized to the distributed nature of blockchain technologies,” he said. “Ultimately, although the individual components in Bascom were known, the court found an inventive concept in the nonconventional and nongeneric arrangement of the known pieces.”

Another case is the Federal Circuit’s 2016 decision in Enfish v. Microsoft. There, the court found two patents on a type of database were not directed to abstract ideas, but rather, a specific improvement to the way computers operate.

With that in mind, Isaacson said it could be helpful for applicants to cast their blockchain-based invention as a solution to a computer-based problem. In other words, they’re providing a technical solution to a technical problem, similar to Enfish.

The Federal Circuit in DDR Holdings v. Hotels.com also found an invention rooted in computer technology that overcomes a problem arising in the realm of computer networks is patent-eligible.

With blockchain, Isaacson said there will be specific technical features — for example, with respect to how to prepare and deploy a smart contract to perform specific functions, or novel and particular ways of preparing, storing and retrieving data on the blockchain — that applicants can claim in their applications and use to argue their invention solves a computer-based problem.

“If you can draft your applications that are blockchain-based with those legal arguments in mind, you give yourself a much better chance of overcoming Alice rejections,” he said.

Overcoming Obviousness

Patent examiners have been reluctant to grant patents for inventions that take a known, pre-internet business process and apply it to the internet. In a similar way, applicants could have trouble taking a known internet-based process and simply applying it on a distributed ledger.

One example may be an invention that takes a land deed and, rather than storing it on a hard drive, simply stores the deed on the blockchain. Without more, this application could be susceptible to a rejection on the grounds that it would be obvious.

“Just taking an application and [putting] in on the blockchain is not going to get you a patent,” Mark Kesslen of Lowenstein Sandler LLP said. “You’re going to be stopped in your tracks on [obviousness].”

According to Isaacson, applications will need to drill down and articulate novel features that are innovated to address problems arising from computer network usage and to make the invention work on the blockchain.

In the example of a land deed, the applicant could identify specific problems in how the internet is used to process land deeds, then outline new blockchain-based technologies that would solve those problems and result in things such as innovative time-saving steps or decreased processing costs.

“You’ve got to dig down a layer or two to have success in getting a patent on that blockchain-based invention,” Isaacson said. “It has got to be more than just taking that same process from the internet and applying it on a distributed ledger.”

Proving Infringement

Blockchain, by its nature, is open and collaborative. This is one of the advantages of the technology — but it can also create issues when trying to prove infringement.

“There are advantages to using a decentralized blockchain architecture, but proving infringement of a claim that includes a decentralized blockchain raises issues of who is infringing and where the infringement occurs,” Riley said.

The type of patent claim that is drafted matters, Riley said. Process claims relate to a series of steps for performing a task. And there are also system claims, which don’t cover the steps but rather the parts that carry out those steps.

To prove infringement of a system claim with a blockchain component, Riley said it will have to be shown the system is put into service and benefit is obtained within the U.S. For infringement of blockchain steps, it would require, among other things, that all the steps be performed in the U.S.

Blockchain patents can also be susceptible to issues of divided infringement. This occurs when the claimed steps of a patented process are performed by different entities.

“The very nature of blockchain requires multiple actors to make it work,” Franklin, the Kilpatrick Townsend attorney, said.

While the Federal Circuit has made it less difficult to prove divided infringement with some recent decisions, it still presents certain challenges. Prospective patent owners may be able to get out in front of these kinds of issues in how they write their patent claims.

Apurv Gaurav of Bookoff McAndrews PLLC suggested one strategy may be to create multiple independent claims, with each claim trying to control one aspect of the blockchain process, whether it be encryption or something like validation of the block.

Franklin said applicants can focus on claiming just the steps performed by one actor.

“I try and figure out what feeder or what choke point only has one party in it,” he said.

This strategy can be effective in blocking competitors from coming up with similar inventions. Franklin analogized the blockchain process to a highway. Even if there isn’t a patent on the highway, everyone has to use an on-ramp to get there.

“So a patent to the on-ramp is just as good as a patent to the main highway,” he said.

Keeping Claims Broad

New blockchain-based platforms continue to emerge as the technology develops. Gaurav said this is something that patent applicants will want to keep in mind when writing their applications.

He suggested applicants keep the written description of their invention, called the specification, broad. Mention the overall benefit that the platform is providing and describe the various steps, but keep it open enough that other platforms can provide these same benefits. This will allow for other blockchain-based platforms to potentially be covered by the patent.

“It broadens your application and doesn’t restrict it to one set of blockchain platforms,” Gaurav said.
